
### TBR1 gene mutation
### **TBR1-Related Neurodevelopmental Disorder Profile**
Disease Overview TBR1-related neurodevelopmental disorder is a condition caused by mutations in the TBR1 gene, which encodes a neuron-specific transcription factor essential for forebrain development and the regulation of laminar and regional neuronal identities1.2. Pathogenic variants in this gene lead to a clinical spectrum that includes intellectual disability (ID), autism spectrum disorder (ASD), and malformations of cortical development (MCD), specifically frontal pachygyria3−5. TBR1 plays a critical role in controlling glutamatergic neuronal cell fate and interacts with other key proteins like CASK and FOXP2 to regulate genes necessary for proper neuronal migration2.6.
Epidemiology/PrevalenceMalformations of cortical development are considered rare diseases, but their exact prevalence is currently unknown7. Mutations in TBR1 have been identified as a rare cause of sporadic cases of autism and intellectual disability1.

How It’s DiagnosedDiagnosis is primarily achieved through next-generation sequencing (NGS), such as trio-based exome sequencing, which identifies de novo mutations (duplications, frameshifts, or missense variants) in the TBR1 gene3,15,16. Clinicians also utilize high-quality brain MRI to detect subtle cortical malformations like mild pachygyria, which may be missed in standard evaluations of intellectual disability3,5,8.
Natural HistoryDevelopmental concerns usually arise in the first year of life, initially presenting as global delay and hypotonia9,11. Motor milestones are significantly delayed; for example, sitting may not occur until 12–28 months, and some patients may never achieve autonomous walking9,11,12. Feeding issues can be severe enough to require the placement of a percutaneous gastrostomy catheter12. While physical growth may initially be low for gestational age, some children show catch-up growth later in childhood11,12.
Therapeutic LandscapeNot addressed in available sources, though patients typically receive supportive care through special education and physical, occupational, and speech therapy10.
