
### Ethylmalonic Encephalopathy
### **Ethylmalonic Encephalopathy (EE) Disease Profile****Disease Overview**
Ethylmalonic encephalopathy (EE) is a severe, autosomal recessive metabolic disorder that primarily affects the brain, gastrointestinal tract, and peripheral blood vessels1.2. It is caused by mutations in the ETHE1 gene located on chromosome 19q13.31, which encodes a mitochondrial matrix protein1.3. This protein functions as a mitochondrial sulfur dioxygenase necessary for the catabolism of sulfide4. When this protein malfunctions, toxic levels of hydrogen sulfide (H2S) and thiosulfate accumulate in tissues, inhibiting critical enzymes like cytochrome c oxidase (COX) and disrupting fatty acid oxidation4.**Epidemiology/Prevalence**
EE is considered a very rare condition; as of 2004, only about 30 cases had been described in medical literature5. However, the actual incidence may be higher because the biochemical symptoms are sometimes misattributed to other metabolic disorders5. Most reported cases involve individuals of Mediterranean or Arab extraction6.**Clinical Features**
The clinical presentation of EE is characterized by a triad of neurological, vascular, and gastrointestinal symptoms:**Diagnosis**
Diagnosis is established through clinical observation, biochemical markers, and genetic testing:**Natural History**
EE is typically a disorder of early infancy, with symptom onset ranging from birth to several months of age, though rare "atypical" cases may present as late as 16 years old2,8,10. The disease is generally progressive, leading to severe neurologic regression7. Death usually occurs within the first decade of life, often due to metabolic decompensation or cardiopulmonary arrest2,7,12.**Therapeutic Landscape**
The therapeutic approach focuses on reducing the accumulation of toxic hydrogen sulfide:**Research (Preclinical & Clinical)**
