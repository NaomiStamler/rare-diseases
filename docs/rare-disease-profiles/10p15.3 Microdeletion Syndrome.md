
# **10p15.3 Microdeletion Syndrome**

### Disease Overview

10p15.3 microdeletion syndrome is a rare genetic condition characterized by the loss of genetic material on the short arm of chromosome 10. The syndrome is typically categorized into two groups: patients with a smaller deletion restricted to the 10p15.3 region and those with a larger deletion involving the 10p15.3–15.1 region. While clinical differences between these groups are not significantly distinct, the smaller deletion region contains critical genes such as ZMYND11 and DIP2C. Loss-of-function mutations in ZMYND11 specifically cause ZMYND11-related syndromic intellectual disability (ZRSID), a disorder where the protein's role as a "chromatin reader" is disrupted, leading to aberrant gene expression during brain development.

### Epidemiology/Prevalence

This is an extremely rare condition; prior to recent research published in 2025, only 26 cases had been reported in medical literature.

### Clinical Features

The clinical presentation of 10p15.3 microdeletion syndrome is diverse and includes:
- Developmental and Intellectual Impact: Global developmental delay (DD), intellectual disability (ID), and language and motor delays.
- Behavioral Symptoms: Hyperactivity, repetitive behaviors, and general behavioral disruptions.
- Physical Characteristics: Abnormal facial features, short stature, and deformities of the hands and feet.
- Neurological Issues: Hypotonia (low muscle tone) and seizures.
- Congenital Heart Defects (CHDs): Observed in approximately 19.2% of patients, often involving genes like DIP2C.

### Diagnosis

Diagnosis is typically achieved through detailed genetic analysis, such as chromosomal microarray or sequencing, to identify a de novo deletion at the 10p15.3 locus. Clinical suspicion may initially lean toward other conditions; for instance, some patients have been originally suspected of having Williams syndrome before genetic testing confirmed the 10p15.3 microdeletion. Diagnosis may also identify specific haploinsufficiency or missense variants of the ZMYND11 gene.

### Therapeutic Landscape

There are currently no disease-specific approved therapies for 10p15.3 microdeletion syndrome or ZRSID 2. Management typically focuses on addressing individual symptoms like seizures or heart defects. However, recent research has identified the KMT2A (MLL1) protein as a potential therapeutic target, as ZMYND11 normally functions to inhibit KMT2A activity. The drug revumenib, which is approved for certain types of leukemia, has shown potential in preclinical models to attenuate gene expression changes caused by ZMYND11 loss.

### Research

Preclinical Research:
- Zebrafish Models: Used to confirm that deficiency of the DIP2C gene leads to heart defects similar to those seen in human patients.
- Mouse Models: Researchers utilized Zmynd11 conditional deletion mice to observe reduced dendritic branching and spine density in neurons, as well as hyperactivity and impaired motor coordination.
- Mechanistic Studies: Studies have discovered that ZMYND11 interacts with the chromatin regulator KMT2A; when ZMYND11 is mutated, this interaction is lost, leading to the upregulation of non-neuronal gene programs.
