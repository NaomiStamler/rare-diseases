
### Alveolar Rhabdomyosarcoma
### **Disease Profile: Alveolar Rhabdomyosarcoma (ARMS)****Disease Overview**
Alveolar rhabdomyosarcoma (ARMS) is a rare and aggressive pediatric soft tissue malignancy that arises from skeletal muscle tissue1.2. It is the second most common subtype of rhabdomyosarcoma (RMS), a cancer that accounts for approximately 3% of all childhood malignant tumors1,3,4. ARMS is characterized by a specific histological pattern and is frequently associated with chromosomal translocations that create oncogenic fusion proteins4.5. Based on risk stratification, ARMS is typically categorized as an intermediate or high-risk disease, with significantly poorer outcomes for high-risk and metastatic cases4.**Epidemiology/Prevalence**
The incidence of RMS in individuals under the age of 20 is approximately 4.5 patients per million in the United States, which translates to about 350 new cases annually2,6. ARMS specifically has a prevalence of approximately 1.0 case per million population7. While RMS follows a bimodal age distribution overall, ARMS tends to occur more frequently in late childhood and adolescence4,6,7. There is a slight male predominance in childhood RMS generally, though specific gender predominance for the ARMS subtype is not always clearly defined6,7. Geographically, incidence rates are stable globally, except in East Asia (Japan, India, and China), where the incidence is lower at approximately 2 cases per million6.**Clinical Features**
ARMS primary tumors are most commonly found on the trunk and extremities, though they can also occur in the inferior orbit of the eye4,7,8. Clinically, ARMS is often more aggressive than the embryonal subtype (ERMS)4. Approximately 15–20% of children diagnosed with RMS have diffused metastatic disease at the time of initial presentation, which is a significant factor in prognostic stratification9. Patients with high-risk ARMS lesions face a 5-year survival rate of less than 30%4,10.**How It’s Diagnosed**
Diagnosis of ARMS involves a combination of histological, immunohistochemical, and molecular techniques:**Natural History**
The natural history of ARMS is marked by aggressive local growth and a high potential for distant metastasis4,9. The disease progression is heavily influenced by fusion status; PAX3-FOXO1 positive cases often occur at an older age and carry a worse prognosis compared to PAX7-FOXO1 positive cases6. Without effective multi-agent chemotherapy, recurrence rates are as high as 50–60%15. For patients who relapse, the 5-year survival rate drops significantly to approximately 17%1,9.**Therapeutic Landscape**
Standard treatment for ARMS requires a multimodal approach involving systemic chemotherapy, surgical resection, and radiation therapy1,16.**Research (Preclinical and Clinical)**
