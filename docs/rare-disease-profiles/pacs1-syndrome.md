
### PACS1 Syndrome
### **PACS1 Syndrome Disease Profile****Disease Overview**
PACS1 syndrome, also known as Schuurs-Hoeijmakers syndrome (SHMS) or PACS1-related neurodevelopmental disorder, is a rare genetic neurodevelopmental disorder1.2. It is primarily characterized by mild-to-moderate intellectual disability, delays in speech and motor skills, and distinct craniofacial features1.3. The condition is caused by pathogenic variants in the PACS1 gene, specifically recurrent variants (c.607C>T and c.608G>A) that typically occur de novo, meaning they are not inherited from the parents4.5. These variants are thought to disrupt protein trafficking within the Golgi apparatus through a gain-of-function or dominant-negative mechanism4.**Epidemiology/Prevalence**
The prevalence of PACS1 syndrome is estimated to be less than 1 per 1,000,000 births2. To date, over 100 individuals have been reported in medical literature, with the condition affecting males and females in approximately equal ratios2. Experts believe the syndrome is likely underdiagnosed2.**Clinical Features**
Patients with PACS1 syndrome exhibit a range of physical and developmental symptoms:**Diagnosis**
Diagnosis is most commonly achieved through exome or genome sequencing6. A specific DNA methylation episignature has been identified, which can help clinicians interpret less common variants of the PACS1 gene6. In some cases, the condition may be suspected prenatally if ultrasound imaging detects congenital heart defects or other suggestive anomalies, leading to follow-up exome sequencing via amniocentesis6.**Natural History**
The onset of symptoms typically occurs during the neonatal period or infancy2. While long-term data are limited because many known patients are still children or young adults, life expectancy does not appear to be reduced7. However, autonomy is often limited, and many affected individuals require lifelong support from caregivers7.**Therapeutic Landscape**
