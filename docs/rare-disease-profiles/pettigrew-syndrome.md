
### Pettigrew syndrome
### Pettigrew Syndrome Disease Profile
Disease OverviewPettigrew syndrome (PGS) is a rare form of X-linked intellectual disability (XLID)1.2. It is caused by loss-of-function mutations in the AP1S2 gene, located on the X chromosome at Xp22.21,3,4. This gene encodes the sigma-1 subunit (σ1B) of the adaptor protein-1 (AP-1) complex, which is essential for clathrin-dependent endocytosis pathways3. PGS is characterized by a combination of cognitive impairment, physical malformations, and progressive neurological symptoms3.5.
Epidemiology/PrevalencePGS is considered a rare cause of XLID6. Research indicates that variants of the AP1S2 gene account for approximately 0.72% of intellectual disability families, with reported prevalence ranging from 0.33% to 1.2% across large cohorts6. A comprehensive literature review identified a total of 51 patients and 11 distinct pathogenic alleles described globally7.8.

Natural HistoryPGS is described as a neuroprogressive or neurodegenerative disorder5,26,27. While some symptoms like hypotonia and hydrocephalus may be present at birth or in early infancy, other features such as limb spasticity and basal ganglia calcification develop and worsen as the patient ages5,15. Motor milestones are significantly delayed; walking is often achieved between ages 1.5 and 8, though many patients eventually progress to a major handicap in adulthood19,20. Facial features also progressively coarsen from childhood into adulthood17,28.
Therapeutic LandscapeNot addressed in available sources. (The sources mention surgical interventions for specific symptoms, such as ventriculoperitoneal shunts for hydrocephalus or cataract surgery, but do not describe a broader therapeutic framework for the syndrome25,29,30).
