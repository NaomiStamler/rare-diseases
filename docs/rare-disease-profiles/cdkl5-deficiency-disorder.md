### CDKL5 Deficiency Disorder (CDD) Disease Profile
Disease OverviewCDKL5 Deficiency Disorder (CDD) is a developmental and epileptic encephalopathy (DEE)1.2. It is caused by pathogenic variants in the CDKL5 gene, located on the X chromosome, which encodes a serine-threonine kinase essential for normal brain development and function3,4. The disorder is characterized by severe early-onset intractable epilepsy and significant disturbances in motor, cognitive, visual, and autonomic functions1. Historically, it was considered a variant of Rett syndrome, but it is now recognized as a distinct clinical entity5,6.
Epidemiology/PrevalenceWhile the exact prevalence in the general population is unknown, the estimated incidence is approximately 2.36 in 100,000 live births5. The disorder affects females more frequently than males, with a female-to-male ratio of approximately 4:11,7. Despite this, the clinical severity can be equivalent between sexes, though phenotypic variation occurs due to factors like X-chromosome inactivation patterns in females and mosaicism in both sexes1,7.
**Clinical Features**
How It’s DiagnosedThe diagnosis is established through molecular genetic testing that identifies a heterozygous (in females) or hemizygous (in males) CDKL5 pathogenic variant18,19.
Natural HistorySymptoms typically emerge in early infancy, with seizures often being the first sign20. Some children (up to 40%) experience a "honeymoon period" around ages one to two where seizure frequency temporarily decreases before returning25. Brain MRIs are often normal in the first year but frequently show progressive cortical and cerebellar atrophy as the child ages13,15,26. Developmental impairments and medical comorbidities, such as GI and sleep issues, are typically lifelong15,16.
Therapeutic LandscapeThere is currently no cure for CDD27. Management is multidisciplinary and focused on supportive care28,29.
