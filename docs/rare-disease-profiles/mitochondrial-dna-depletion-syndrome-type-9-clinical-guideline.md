
### Mitochondrial DNA Depletion Syndrome Type 9: Clinical Guideline
### Disease Profile: Mitochondrial DNA Depletion Syndrome Type 9 (MTDPS9)Disease Overview
Mitochondrial DNA depletion syndrome 9 (MTDPS9), also known as SUCLG1-related mitochondrial DNA depletion syndrome, is a rare and severe neurometabolic disorder that primarily affects infants1−3. It is characterized by encephalomyopathy, a condition impacting the brain, spinal cord, and musclesBull 2.3. The disease is caused by pathogenic variants (mutations) in the SUCLG1 gene, which provides instructions for an enzyme essential for maintaining mitochondrial DNA (mtDNA)4.5. When mtDNA is depleted, cells cannot produce energy efficiently, leading to the characteristic symptoms of the disorder6.Epidemiology/Prevalence
MTDPS9 is an extremely rare condition, with approximately 40 cases reported in medical literature5. It is inherited in an autosomal recessive pattern, meaning an affected child must inherit two copies of the mutated gene, one from each biological parent7.8. Carriers, who have only one mutated gene, typically do not show symptoms7.8.Clinical Features

The progression of MTDPS9 is typically severe and rapid. Symptoms appear shortly after birth3,19. Affected individuals face serious health complications and a shortened lifespan, usually not surviving past childhood19. A specific, severe form known as fatal infantile lactic acidosis can result in a toxic buildup of acid within the first few days of life, with survival often limited to only a few days after birth19.Therapeutic Landscape
There are currently no FDA-approved treatments specifically for MTDPS920. Management focuses on symptom management and supportive care through a coordinated healthcare team20,21. Patient organizations like the United Mitochondrial Disease Foundation (UMDF) and MitoAction offer resources for connecting with experts and finding support22,23.Research
