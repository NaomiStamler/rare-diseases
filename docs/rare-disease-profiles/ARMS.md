# **Alveolar Rhabdomyosarcoma (ARMS)**

### Disease Overview

Alveolar rhabdomyosarcoma (ARMS) is a rare and aggressive pediatric soft tissue malignancy that arises from skeletal muscle tissue. It is the second most common subtype of rhabdomyosarcoma (RMS), a cancer that accounts for approximately 3% of all childhood malignant tumors. ARMS is characterized by a specific histological pattern and is frequently associated with chromosomal translocations that create oncogenic fusion proteins. Based on risk stratification, ARMS is typically categorized as an intermediate or high-risk disease, with significantly poorer outcomes for high-risk and metastatic cases.

### Epidemiology/Prevalence

The incidence of RMS in individuals under the age of 20 is approximately 4.5 patients per million in the United States, which translates to about 350 new cases annually. ARMS specifically has a prevalence of approximately 1.0 case per million population. While RMS follows a bimodal age distribution overall, ARMS tends to occur more frequently in late childhood and adolescence. There is a slight male predominance in childhood RMS generally, though specific gender predominance for the ARMS subtype is not always clearly defined. Geographically, incidence rates are stable globally, except in East Asia (Japan, India, and China), where the incidence is lower at approximately 2 cases per million.

### Clinical Features

ARMS primary tumors are most commonly found on the trunk and extremities, though they can also occur in the inferior orbit of the eye. Clinically, ARMS is often more aggressive than the embryonal subtype (ERMS). Approximately 15–20% of children diagnosed with RMS have diffused metastatic disease at the time of initial presentation, which is a significant factor in prognostic stratification. Patients with high-risk ARMS lesions face a 5-year survival rate of less than 30%.

### How It's Diagnosed

Diagnosis of ARMS involves a combination of histological, immunohistochemical, and molecular techniques:

- Histology: ARMS is identified by densely packed, small, round cells that line septations, creating a pattern that resembles fetal alveoli
- Immunohistochemistry: The presence of skeletal muscle proteins—such as alpha-actin, MyoD1, myogenin, and desmin—is used to differentiate ARMS from other small round blue cell tumors like neuroblastoma or Ewing sarcoma
- Molecular Biology: The majority (80%) of ARMS cases are "fusion-positive," involving a balanced translocation between chromosomes 2 and 13 (PAX3-FOXO1, found in 60% of cases) or chromosomes 1 and 13 (PAX7-FOXO1, found in 20% of cases). Identifying these fusions is critical, as fusion-negative ARMS often behaves clinically more like the more favorable ERMS subtype
- Imaging: Diagnostic procedures used in clinical settings and trials include Computed Tomography (CT), Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI), Positron Emission Tomography (PET), and bone scans

### Natural History

The natural history of ARMS is marked by aggressive local growth and a high potential for distant metastasis. The disease progression is heavily influenced by fusion status; PAX3-FOXO1 positive cases often occur at an older age and carry a worse prognosis compared to PAX7-FOXO1 positive cases. Without effective multi-agent chemotherapy, recurrence rates are as high as 50–60%. For patients who relapse, the 5-year survival rate drops significantly to approximately 17%.

### Therapeutic Landscape

Standard treatment for ARMS requires a multimodal approach involving systemic chemotherapy, surgical resection, and radiation therapy.

- Chemotherapy: In North America, the standard "VAC" regimen consists of vincristine, dactinomycin, and cyclophosphamide. European centers often use a similar "IVA" regime (ifosfamide, vincristine, and actinomycin D). Maintenance therapy with vinorelbine and low-dose cyclophosphamide has shown promise in improving survival for high-risk patients
- Surgery: Complete surgical resection with margins of 0.5–1 cm is preferred for localized disease, though the extent of surgery must be balanced against functional and cosmetic outcomes in children
- Radiation: Radiotherapy (including intensity-modulated radiation therapy and proton beam therapy) or brachytherapy is used for local control, particularly in cases with microscopic or gross residual disease

### Research (Preclinical and Clinical)

Preclinical Research:

- Animal Models: Researchers utilize cell-line-derived xenografts (CDX), patient-derived xenografts (PDX), and genetically engineered mouse models (GEMM) to study drug mechanisms and tumor maintenance. Zebrafish models are also used for high-throughput drug screening and tracking metastasis
- Molecular Targets: Studies are exploring the role of the Unfolded Protein Response (UPR), specifically the IRE1 and PERK pathways, which are constitutively active in ARMS and support cell survival. Targeting autophagy and apoptosis pathways (such as Bcl-2 family proteins or PLK1) is another area of active investigation to overcome chemoresistance

Clinical Research (Pediatric Focus):

- NCT06669013: A recruiting trial evaluating chemo-immunotherapy (dinutuximab beta) in patients under 18 with advanced/metastatic GD2-positive RMS
- NCT04994132: A phase III trial comparing the addition of vinorelbine to the VAC backbone and the use of maintenance therapy for high-risk RMS
- NCT05304585: A phase III trial for newly diagnosed patients, examining centralized molecular risk stratification and therapy intensification for mutated cases
- NCT02748135: A completed study of TB-403 (anti-PlGF antibody) in pediatric subjects with relapsed or refractory ARMS
- EudraCT 2022-002793-91: A prospective phase I/II trial of an individualized peptide vaccine for pediatric and AYA patients with metastasized fusion-driven sarcomas, including ARMS
