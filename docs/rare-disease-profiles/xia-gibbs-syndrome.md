
### Xia-Gibbs Syndrome
### **Xia-Gibbs Syndrome (XGS) Disease Profile****Disease Overview**
Xia-Gibbs syndrome (XGS) is a rare autosomal dominant neurodevelopmental disorder caused by a heterozygous pathogenic variant in the AHDC1 gene on chromosome 1p361−3. The syndrome was first described in 2014 and is characterized primarily by global developmental delay, significant speech impairment, and structural brain anomalies1.4. The underlying mechanism of the disease is still being studied, but current data suggests a dominant-negative model, though haploinsufficiency (loss of function) has not been ruled out5.**Epidemiology/Prevalence**
The prevalence of XGS is estimated to be at least one in 80,000 live births6. To date, approximately 280 individuals have been identified and reported worldwide6.7. Experts suggest the actual incidence may be higher, as many individuals remain undiagnosed due to a lack of access to advanced genomic testing6.**Clinical Features**
XGS presents with a broad spectrum of clinical manifestations, ranging from core neurodevelopmental issues to systemic physical anomalies:**How It’s Diagnosed**
Diagnosis is established through molecular genetic testing to identify a heterozygous pathogenic variant in AHDC116,18.**Natural History**
The long-term natural history of XGS is still being defined. It is known that survival into adulthood is possible, with at least one reported individual alive at age 5521.**Therapeutic Landscape**
There is currently no cure for XGS; management is symptomatic and supportive18.**Research**
Current research is heavily focused on understanding the function of the AHDC1 gene and its role in the syndrome's pathology.
