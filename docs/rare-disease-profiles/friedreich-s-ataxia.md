
### Friedreich's Ataxia
### Disease Profile: Friedreich's Ataxia (FA)**Disease Overview**
Friedreich's ataxia is a rare autosomal recessive neurodegenerative disorder characterized as a multisystem disease1. It is primarily caused by a homozygous GAA repeat expansion in the first intron of the FXN gene on chromosome 9, which leads to a deficiency of the mitochondrial protein frataxin1.2. Frataxin is essential for iron homeostasis, iron–sulphur cluster biogenesis, and cellular redox reactions; its deficiency results in impaired mitochondrial energy production (ATP), iron accumulation, and increased oxidative stress, ultimately leading to cell death3.4.**Epidemiology and Prevalence**
FA is the most prevalent inherited ataxia5.**Clinical Features**
Friedreich's ataxia affects multiple systems, including the nervous, cardiac, musculoskeletal, and metabolic systems1.**Diagnosis**
Diagnosis is established through a combination of clinical presentation and genetic testing to identify FXN mutations9.**Natural History**
The disease is slowly progressive and life-limiting9.**Therapeutic Landscape****Research (Preclinical and Clinical)**
**Preclinical Research:**Studies in mouse models have shown that frataxin deficiency leads to obesity and metabolic dysfunction, while treatment with butyrate has shown potential in dampening neuroinflammation by initiating antioxidant responses in microglia20,21.
