
### Fliedner-Zweier syndrome
### Disease Profile: Fliedner-Zweier Syndrome (FZS)**Disease Overview**
Fliedner-Zweier syndrome (FZS) is an exceptionally rare autosomal dominant neurodevelopmental disorder caused by heterozygous variants in the SCAF4 gene, located on chromosome 21q22.111−3. The SCAF4 gene encodes a protein that acts as an mRNA anti-terminator, which is critical for ensuring proper mRNA maturation and preventing the production of nonfunctional, truncated proteins2,4,5. Mutations in this gene lead to impaired mRNA processing and widespread transcriptional dysregulation, which underpins the syndrome's complex clinical manifestations2,6,7.**Epidemiology/Prevalence**
FZS is considered an exceptionally rare condition3. As of the most recent clinical review, only 69 cases have been documented worldwide3. The syndrome shows a notable male predominance, with approximately 66% of reported cases being male (45 males to 23 females)8.9. Most identified mutations are de novo (56%), though paternal inheritance has been documented in specific cases8,10.**Clinical Features**
The clinical presentation of FZS is diverse, primarily affecting neurodevelopment and several organ systems:**How It’s Diagnosed**
Diagnosis is primarily achieved through advanced genetic testing:**Natural History**
The onset of symptoms typically occurs in early childhood, with 87% of patients diagnosed before the age of 18; however, age of onset can range from 20 months to 47 years8,9. While motor milestones (such as walking) may sometimes develop within normal timeframes, language development is frequently severely delayed14. Intellectual impairment and behavioral challenges are persistent features, and while seizures are common, they are not present in every case10,14,15.**Therapeutic Landscape**
There is currently no cure for Fliedner-Zweier syndrome; management focuses on symptom-specific interventions:**Research (Preclinical and Clinical)**
