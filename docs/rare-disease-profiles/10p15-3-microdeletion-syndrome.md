
# **10p15.3 Microdeletion Syndrome**

10p15.3 Microdeletion Syndrome (including ZMYND11)

## Disease Overview

10p15.3 microdeletion syndrome is a rare genetic condition characterized by the loss of genetic material on the short arm of chromosome 101. The syndrome is typically categorized into two groups: patients with a smaller deletion restricted to the 10p15.3 region and those with a larger deletion involving the 10p15.3–15.1 region1. While clinical differences between these groups are not significantly distinct, the smaller deletion region contains critical genes such as ZMYND11 and DIP2C1. Loss-of-function mutations in ZMYND11 specifically cause ZMYND11-related syndromic intellectual disability (ZRSID), a disorder where the protein's role as a "chromatin reader" is disrupted, leading to aberrant gene expression during brain development.

## Epidemiology/Prevalence

This is an extremely rare condition; prior to recent research published in 2025, only 26 cases had been reported in medical literature.

## Clinical Features

Diagnosis is typically achieved through detailed genetic analysis, such as chromosomal microarray or sequencing, to identify a de novo deletion at the 10p15.3 locus1. Clinical suspicion may initially lean toward other conditions; for instance, some patients have been originally suspected of having Williams syndrome before genetic testing confirmed the 10p15.3 microdeletion1. Diagnosis may also identify specific haploinsufficiency or missense variants of the ZMYND11 gene.

## Natural History
Not addressed in available sources. While sources describe symptoms present in early childhood (such as a case report of a 1-year-old male), they do not provide a comprehensive longitudinal account of the disease progression across a full human lifespan.

## Therapeutic Landscape

There are currently no disease-specific approved therapies for 10p15.3 microdeletion syndrome or ZRSID2. Management typically focuses on addressing individual symptoms like seizures or heart defects1. However, recent research has identified the KMT2A (MLL1) protein as a potential therapeutic target, as ZMYND11 normally functions to inhibit KMT2A activity2,5. The drug revumenib, which is approved for certain types of leukemia, has shown potential in preclinical models to attenuate gene expression changes caused by ZMYND11 loss2,5.
