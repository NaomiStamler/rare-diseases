# **Surfactant Protein B (SP-B) Deficiency**

### Disease Overview

Surfactant protein B (SP-B) deficiency is a rare inherited disorder that causes interstitial lung disease. It is characterized by the body's inability to produce functional surfactant—a mixture of fats and proteins that reduces surface tension in the lungs to prevent alveolar collapse during exhalation. The condition is caused by variants in the SFTPB gene and is inherited in an autosomal recessive pattern, meaning an affected child must inherit a disease-causing gene variant from each parent. Synonyms for the condition include SP-B deficiency, pulmonary surfactant metabolism dysfunction-1 (SMDP1), and congenital pulmonary alveolar proteinosis.

### Epidemiology/Prevalence

In the United States, the estimated incidence of surfactant B protein deficiency is 1 in 1 million live births.

### Clinical Features

Infants with SP-B deficiency may appear healthy immediately after birth, but they typically develop severe respiratory distress within hours. Characteristic features include:

- Tachypnea (increased breathing rate)
- Cyanosis (gray or blue skin pallor)
- Grunting and nasal flaring
- Increased oxygen demand and breathing effort

While most cases are severe, there are reports of milder lung disease in children with variants that cause only a partial deficiency.

### Diagnosis

A diagnosis is suspected in infants whose respiratory distress does not improve after five days of life.

- Definitive Diagnosis: Confirmed through genetic testing to identify variants in the SFTPB gene
- Supportive Testing: High-resolution CT scans of the chest, bronchoscopy with bronchoalveolar lavage (lung washing), infant pulmonary function testing, and lung biopsies are used to aid the diagnosis
- Prenatal Testing: Possible during future pregnancies via amniotic fluid or chorionic villus sampling

### Natural History

The condition is extremely serious and progresses rapidly. Without a lung transplant, the disease usually leads to fatal respiratory failure within three to six months of life. Interventions such as surfactant replacement therapy typically only provide temporary relief, as the symptoms continue to worsen over time.

### Therapeutic Landscape

Currently, no specific medical treatments exist for SP-B deficiency.

- Definitive Treatment: Lung transplantation is the only definitive therapy, though it carries high risks for severe health problems in infants
- Supportive Care: Management focuses on symptom monitoring and temporary measures. Surfactant replacement therapy may offer short-term stabilization
- Off-label/Experimental Stabilizers: In children with milder forms, corticosteroids and hydroxychloroquine have been used to attempt to stabilize the disease until a transplant can be performed

### Research

Preclinical Research: Not addressed in available sources.

Clinical Research (Pediatric Focus):

- NCT04056741: A pilot study explored the feasibility and safety of administering surfactant (Infasurf®) through a new supraglottic device in neonates weighing 500 to 5000 grams, including those with SP-B deficiency. This study is currently listed as suspended
- NCT00828243: A completed observational study investigated the genetic regulation of surfactant deficiency by linking specific genetic code changes in SP-B, SP-C, and ABCA3 genes to disrupted surfactant metabolism in newborn infants
