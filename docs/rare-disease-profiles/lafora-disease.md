
### Lafora Disease
### **Lafora Disease Profile****Disease Overview**
Lafora disease (LD) is a fatal, teenage-onset progressive myoclonus epilepsy inherited in an autosomal recessive fashion1.2. It is primarily caused by mutations in either the EPM2A gene, which encodes the glycogen phosphatase laforin, or the EPM2B gene, which encodes the ubiquitin E3 ligase malin3.4. These mutations result in the formation of insufficiently branched, long-chained glycogen that precipitates into insoluble polyglucosan bodies known as Lafora bodies3,5. These bodies accumulate in the brain and other organs, driving neuroinflammation, neurodegeneration, and severe epilepsy3.6.**Epidemiology and Prevalence**
LD is categorized as a rare "orphan" disease2.7. Its frequency is estimated to be no higher than 1 per 100,000 individuals2.**Clinical Features**

Diagnosis often begins with the evaluation of a teenager suspected of having Juvenile Myoclonic Epilepsy (JME), but LD is distinguished through specific tests2:**Natural History**
Symptoms of LD are inexorably progressive2. Following the onset of seizures in the early teens, patients typically reach a vegetative state and succumb to the disease within ten years2,8.
