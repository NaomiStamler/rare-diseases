
### IQSEC2
### IQSEC2 Disease Profile
Disease Overview IQSEC2 is an ultra-rare genetic neurodevelopmental disorder first identified in 20151.2. It is caused by pathological mutations in the IQSEC2 gene, which encodes a protein of 1488 amino acids found at the post-synaptic density (PSD) of both excitatory and inhibitory neurons3. This protein plays a critical role in controlling synaptic transmission, E/I (excitatory/inhibitory) balance, and memory consolidation by mediating AMPA receptor trafficking and dendritic spine maturation3. Mutations in this gene account for approximately 2–5% of all children presenting with frequent seizures, severe intellectual disability, and autism spectrum disorder (ASD)3.
Epidemiology/PrevalenceThe disease is estimated to affect fewer than 1,000 people worldwide1.4. In Spain, approximately 16 to 17 families have been identified1,5,6. Orphanet lists the prevalence as less than 1 per 1,000,0007. The condition follows an X-linked recessive inheritance pattern; because it is linked to the X chromosome, males are generally affected more severely than females, as females possess a second X chromosome that may lack the mutation6−8.

How it’s DiagnosedDiagnosis is typically achieved through genetic testing to identify mutations in the IQSEC2 gene2,8. Clinically, it is categorized under ORPHAcode 397933 and OMIM 3011647. Families often navigate a complex diagnostic process supported by specialized neuropediatric units and rare disease associations12,13.
Natural HistoryThe disorder is characterized by an onset in the neonatal or infancy period7. The microcephaly associated with the syndrome is progressive and develops postnatally10. Due to the severity of the intellectual disability and motor impairments, affected individuals typically require 24-hour care and remain totally dependent for the duration of their lives3,14.
Therapeutic LandscapeCurrently, there is no specific treatment or cure for IQSEC21,6,14. Management is symptomatic and focuses on supportive therapies, such as physical therapy for motor issues and standard (though often ineffective) protocols for managing refractory epilepsy3,9,12.
