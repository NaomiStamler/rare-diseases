    CDKL5 Deficiency Disorder

    Disease Overview

    CDKL5 Deficiency Disorder (CDD) is a developmental and epileptic encephalopathy (DEE). It is caused by pathogenic variants in the CDKL5 gene, located on the X chromosome, which encodes a serine-threonine kinase essential for normal brain development and function. The disorder is characterized by severe early-onset intractable epilepsy and significant disturbances in motor, cognitive, visual, and autonomic functions. Historically, it was considered a variant of Rett syndrome, but it is now recognized as a distinct clinical entity

    Epidemiology/Prevalence

    While the exact prevalence in the general population is unknown, the estimated incidence is approximately 2.36 in 100,000 live births. The disorder affects females more frequently than males, with a female-to-male ratio of approximately 4:1. Despite this, the clinical severity can be equivalent between sexes, though phenotypic variation occurs due to factors like X-chromosome inactivation patterns in females and mosaicism in both sexes

    Clinical Features

    • Epilepsy: Typically begins within the first two months of life (up to 12 months) and is usually severe and refractory to standard medications Seizure types vary and include infantile spasms, tonic, and focal seizures
    • Developmental Delay: All affected individuals have impaired gross motor, fine motor, and speech-language development. Approximately 20% achieve independent walking and 20% develop spoken language.
    • Cerebral Visual Impairment (CVI): Affects 80% of individuals, manifesting as abnormal eye movements (nystagmus), poor tracking, and abnormal responsiveness to light.
    • Movement Disorders: Includes hand and leg stereotypies (e.g., mouthing, flapping), chorea, and dystonia.
    • Autonomic & Gastrointestinal: Common issues include sleep disturbances (90%), constipation, and gastroesophageal reflux disease (GERD).
    • Musculoskeletal: Generalized hypotonia is common, often leading to scoliosis or joint abnormalities.

    Diagnosis

    The diagnosis is established through molecular genetic testing that identifies a heterozygous (in females) or hemizygous (in males) CDKL5 pathogenic variant. - Suggestive Findings: Clinicians should suspect CDD in infants with motor/cognitive delays and epilepsy onset within the first year - Testing Approaches: Multigene panels for epilepsy are the most common diagnostic tool, with sequence analysis identifying about 84% of variants and deletion/duplication analysis identifying the remaining 16% - Advanced Testing: If initial panels are negative, exome or genome sequencing may be used to find rare intronic or noncoding variants

    Natural History

    Symptoms typically emerge in early infancy, with seizures often being the first sign. Some children (up to 40%) experience a "honeymoon period" around ages one to two where seizure frequency temporarily decreases before returning. Brain MRIs are often normal in the first year but frequently show progressive cortical and cerebellar atrophy as the child ages. Developmental impairments and medical comorbidities, such as GI and sleep issues, are typically lifelong

    Therapeutic Landscape

    There is currently no cure for CDD. Management is multidisciplinary and focused on supportive care. - Targeted Therapy: Ganaxolone (Ztalmy®) is the first and only FDA-approved targeted therapy specifically for seizures associated with CDD in patients aged two years and older. - Standard Management: Refractory seizures are managed with various anti-seizure medications (ASMs), though they are often ineffective. - Supportive Care: Therapies include PT/OT, speech-language therapy, and nutritional support; approximately 30% of individuals require a gastrostomy tube for feeding. - Other Interventions: The ketogenic diet and vagus nerve stimulation have been used with variable success for seizure management.

    Research (Preclinical and Clinical)

    Preclinical: Research focuses on understanding the kinase function of CDKL5 and its role in excitatory synapse stability. Protein and gene replacement therapies are currently under investigation

    Clinical (Childhood-Focused): - Ganaxolone: A completed Phase 3 trial (NCT03572933) demonstrated efficacy in children and young adults. A new interventional trial is recruiting/planned for children aged 6 months to 2 years (NCT05249556). - Fenfluramine: Clinical studies (NCT03861871, NCT05064878) have investigated its effect on convulsive seizures in CDD. - Soticlestat: The Phase 2 ARCADE study (NCT03694275) included pediatric participants with CDD to evaluate seizure reduction. - Observational Research: Several ongoing studies and registries (NCT05558371, NCT04486768, NCT05373719) are tracking the natural history and long-term outcomes of the disorder to inform future treatments. - Novel Interventions: Studies are evaluating hyperthermic baths (NCT06447675, NCT07602205) and gut-targeted dietary supplements (NCT06448663) for seizure control.
