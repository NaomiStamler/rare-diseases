    Severe Combined Immunodeficiency (SCID)

    Disease Overview

    Severe combined immunodeficiency (SCID) is a rare and life-threatening genetic disorder that severely impairs the immune system's ability to defend the body against infections. Often referred to as "bubble boy" disease, it results from genetic mutations—of which over 20 are known—that disrupt the development and function of immune cells, primarily T cells and B cells. Because the immune system is virtually non-existent, infants with SCID are highly susceptible to recurrent, severe infections from viruses, bacteria, and fungi. It is considered a pediatric emergency because it is fatal without prompt treatment.

    Epidemiology/Prevalence

    • Incidence: Historically estimated at 1:75,000 to 1:100,000 individuals. However, following the implementation of newborn screening in the USA, the incidence has been found to be higher, approximately 1:58,000 births
    • Common Forms: The most common form is X-linked SCID (SCID-X1), which accounts for approximately 40–50% of all cases. Other common types include those caused by mutations in the ADA, IL7RA, and RAG1 genes
    • Regional Data: In Latin America, the lack of universal neonatal screening in many countries often leads to underdiagnosis and delayed referral.

    Clinical Features

    The clinical presentation typically occurs in the first few months of life. Key features include:

    • Recurrent/Severe Infections: Persistent respiratory tract infections, pneumonia (often caused by Pneumocystis jirovecii or CMV), and disseminated infections from live vaccines like BCG
    • Gastrointestinal Issues: Chronic diarrhea and persistent oral or intestinal candidiasis (thrush)
    • Failure to Thrive: Infants usually have a normal birth weight but deviate from the expected growth curve once infections begin
    • Skin Manifestations: Persistent skin rashes; specific forms like Omenn syndrome may present with generalized erythematous rash, enlarged lymph nodes, and hepatosplenomegaly

    Diagnosis

    • Newborn Screening (NBS): The primary method for early identification, utilizing the quantification of T-cell receptor excision circles (TRECs) from dried blood spots to detect T-cell lymphopenia
    • Laboratory Tests:
    • Complete Blood Count: A lymphocyte count below 2.8 x 10⁹/L in childhood strongly suggests SCID
    • Flow Cytometry: Essential for identifying the specific immunological phenotype (e.g., T-B+NK- or T-B-NK+) based on the presence or absence of lymphocyte subtypes
    • Genetic Testing: Next-generation sequencing is used to identify the specific genetic mutation, which is critical for tailoring treatment
    • Diagnostic Criteria: Patients are categorized into Typical SCID (very low T cells, <0.05 x 10⁹/L), Leaky/Atypical SCID (reduced but present T cells with impaired function), or Omenn Syndrome

    Natural History

    Without definitive treatment, classic SCID is universally fatal, with most patients succumbing to severe opportunistic infections before their first birthday. Early diagnosis through screening significantly improves the prognosis by allowing for intervention before the onset of life-threatening complications.

    Therapeutic Landscape

    • Hematopoietic Stem Cell Transplantation (HSCT): The primary curative treatment. Success rates are highest (over 90%) when performed within the first 3.5 months of life and when an HLA-matched sibling donor is available
    • Enzyme Replacement Therapy (ERT): For patients with ADA-SCID, weekly injections of PEG-ADA (Adagen) can restore some immunity but are not a permanent cure and require lifelong administration
    • Gene Therapy: Explored as a promising alternative for patients lacking a matched donor. It involves modifying the patient's own stem cells to express a functional copy of the defective gene

    Research

    Preclinical Research: Investigation is ongoing into safer viral vectors (transitioning from gammaretroviral to lentiviral vectors) to reduce the risk of insertional mutagenesis, which previously caused leukemia in some trials. Other areas include gene editing (e.g., CRISPR/Cas9), exon skipping using antisense oligonucleotides, and readthrough therapy for nonsense mutations.

    Clinical Research (Focus on Children):

    • X-Linked SCID: Trials are evaluating lentiviral gene transfer in newly diagnosed infants (NCT01512888) and base-edited stem cell transplants in those aged 3 and older (NCT06851767)
    • ADA-SCID: Several studies (NCT03765632, NCT02999984) have assessed the efficacy of a cryopreserved formulation of gene-modified stem cells (OTL-101) in children up to 18 years old
    • Artemis-Deficient SCID: A trial is currently recruiting newly diagnosed infants to test autologous gene therapy using a lentiviral vector (NCT03538899)
    • RAG1-Deficient SCID: Clinical trials are enrolling children up to 24 months of age for autologous stem cell gene therapy (NCT04797260, EudraCT 2019-002343-14)
